SailAway Project - 'The Problem Being Solved.'

Solar Sail research is done by a variety of people using tools that range from home grown to sophisticated mathematical and engineering analysis. While some of the end results of this research do get published and survive the ups and downs of any one person's interest in the subject, the tools themselves rarely get traded or improved unless two like minded researchers can find each other and not violate the intellectual property laws that govern their work.

For the state of the art to advance more efficiently, the tools, intermediate research, and tested solutions to posed problems must survive in a knowledge base accessible to all others interested in solar sails. The demise of solar sail projects must not lead to the demise of the knowledge gained by those who support the project.

SailAway Project - 'Why Solve The Problem?'

A careful reading of the solar sail literature shows that the subject matter covered by theorists proposing solar sail missions is generally confined to a few mission types. These missions can be modelled by current tools or minor improvements on older tools.

Solar Sail mission planners spend considerable time rebuilding necessary analysis tools for non-impulsive, long duration flights and extended, filmy spacecraft structures or they make strong flight or structural restrictions and use simpler planning tools.

Many flight plans are developed as intermediate results resulting from optimization attempts or solution space searches along the way to a different published result.

Duplication of effort, restricted research, and the lack of availability of a body of knowledge representing what is and isn't possible leads to inefficiencies in the research community. These inefficiencies are found among the flight planners, the spacecraft engineers, and the science and commercial customers who might actually fly instruments on a sail.

Solving this problem reduces research inefficiencies and further enables those wishing to promote the use of solar sails for solar system transportation.

SailAway Requirements Specification - 'A Case Study'

We are intent on developing an automated tool for the simulation of structure and flight of solar sails in the inner solar system. This tool will enable researchers to record sail designs, initial conditions, and expected final conditions for simulation and return to them ephemerides, structural histories, and optimization choices that result from the simulation run they started. The tool will also maintain a library of prior simulations in order to support solution space searches by researchers and faster response times for casual browsers.

When a researcher first plans a simulation, they record the details concerning the structure of their sail and the initial internal conditions into the tool or choose an already described sail stored in the library. After choosing or describing a sail the researcher describes the initial external conditions and the desired final conditions for their sail. Both condition sets may be choosen from examples stored in the library. The initial condition set is optional for simulations designed to determine the internal structure of a sail without concern for external conditions beyond force sources. The final condition set is optional for simulations with no flight end boundry conditions.

The tool will verify whether the sail description and initial and final conditions are physically possible or meaningful and reject those that are not. External initial and final conditions are defined in terms of physical properties external to the sail that describe the physics of a flight such as position, velocity, accelertion, momentum, angular momentum and other sources of force upon the sail besides solar gravity and photons. Internal initial conditions are defined in terms describing the physical properties of the sail that do not depend on external references such as sail fabric reflectivity, emmissivity, spar strengths, and attenna look angles.

Once a simulation plan has been established, the researcher defining it may run it. The tool will make use of whatever resources are registered as available on the net and that it deems necessary to produce the results requested by the researcher. The researcher will be able to check up on the state of their simulation periodically by submitting a query to the tool. When so requested, the tool will gather a snapshot of the simulation and deliver it to the researcher.

During a simulation, a researcher may interact with their simulation through an established set of commands available to them through a user interface. The researcher may also pre-plan a set of commands by defining a macro to be used by the simulation instead of a human interaction through their user interface. Macros will be able to support time based commands and simple what-if conditional commands.

Assuming the simulation encounters no physical impossibilities, it will run to completion with or without human interaction and then deliver its results to the address specified by the researcher and to the library for future use.
